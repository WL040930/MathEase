Smart Tips

  • Each term in a linear equation must have a same unit.

  • It is unnecessary to write the unit while forming linear equation.

Figure 1.1.2.1: Method to Form Linear Equations


Example

(a) You are planning a road trip and need to calculate the total distance you will travel. Let's say the distance to your first destination is "d" miles. The distance to your second destination is 20 miles more than twice the distance to the first destination. Finally, the distance to your third destination is 30 miles less than three times the distance to the second destination. Write an algebraic expression to represent the total distance you will travel in terms of "d".

Step 1: Define the variables:

  • Let (d) represent the distance to the first destination in miles.
  • The second destination is (2d + 20) miles away (20 miles more than twice the first distance).
  • The third destination is (3 × (2d + 20) - 30) miles away (30 miles less than three times the second distance).

Step 2: Write the algebraic expression for the total distance:

  • The total distance is the sum of the distances to all three destinations.
  • So, the expression is [d + (2d + 20) + (3 × (2d + 20) - 30)].
  • Simplifying, we get [d + 2d + 20 + 6d + 60 - 30].
  • Combining like terms, the expression becomes (9d + 50).

Step 3: Final expression:

  • The final algebraic expression representing the total distance you will travel is (9d + 50).
  • This expression gives us the total distance based on the distance to the first destination represented by (d).

Three Methods to Solve Linear Equations

  • Trial and Improvement Method: Making educated guesses and refining them until the correct solution is found.

  • Application of Equality Concept: Using operations to both sides of an equation equally to isolate and solve for the variable.

  • Backtracking Method: Starting from a guessed solution and working backward to verify if it satisfies the equation, adjusting as needed.

Example

(a) You're planning a community bake sale and need to calculate the total profit from selling cookies and muffins. Define two variables to represent the number of cookies and the number of muffins you'll sell, respectively.

Step 1: Identify the quantities:

  • Quantity 1: Number of cookies.
  • Quantity 2: Number of muffins.

Step 2: Define the variables:

  • Let c represent the number of cookies you'll sell.
  • Let m represent the number of muffins you'll sell.

Step 3: Define the algebraic expression:

  • Suppose the profit from one cookie is $2.00 and the profit from one muffin is $3.00.
  • The total profit (P) can be represented as: P = 2.00c + 3.00m.

Step 4: Determine the total profit using three methods:

  • Trial and Improvement Method:
    Guess and check different values for c and m until the desired profit is achieved.
    Example: If you aim for a profit of $50, try c = 10 and m = 10.
    P = 2.00(10) + 3.00(10) = 20 + 30 = $50. The guess is correct.

  • Application of Equality Concept:
    Set up an equation and solve for one variable in terms of the other.
    Example: If the desired profit is $50 and you know you will sell 10 cookies, find m.
    50 = 2.00(10) + 3.00m
    50 = 20 + 3.00m
    30 = 3.00m
    m = 10. Thus, you need to sell 10 muffins.

  • Backtracking Method:
    Start with an assumed solution and check if it satisfies the equation.
    Example: Assume you sell 8 cookies and 11 muffins, check the total profit.
    P = 2.00(8) + 3.00(11) = 16 + 33 = $49, which is close but not the target. Adjust and try again.

keyboard_arrow_right 6.2.1 What are linear equations in two variables?

Smart Tips

  • Linear equations in two variables are algebraic equations of the form ax + by = c, where a, b, and c are constants, and x and y are variables. The solution to such an equation is the ordered pair (x, y) that makes the equation true, representing a point on the graph of the equation.

Figure 6.2.1.1: Linear Equations in Two Variables

Example

(a) Imagine you are managing a small business and need to calculate the total revenue from selling two products: T-shirts and hoodies. Define two variables to represent the number of T-shirts and the number of hoodies sold, respectively, and write the revenue equations. Then, demonstrate how to add and subtract these equations.

Step 1: Define the variables:

  • Let x represent the number of T-shirts sold.
  • Let y represent the number of hoodies sold.

Step 2: Write the revenue equations:

  • Revenue from T-shirts: R1 = 20x (assuming each T-shirt sells for $20)
  • Revenue from hoodies: R2 = 50y (assuming each hoodie sells for $50)

Step 3: Add the revenue equations:

Combine the equations to find the total revenue:

  • Total Revenue: R = R1 + R2
  • R = 20x + 50y

Step 4: Subtract the revenue equations:

Subtract one equation from the other to analyze differences in revenue sources:

  • Let's subtract revenue from T-shirts from the total revenue equation:
  • R - R1 = (20x + 50y) - 20x
  • R - R1 = 50y

keyboard_arrow_right 6.2.2 How do you form linear equations in two variables?

Smart Tips

  • To form linear equations in two variables, identify the changing quantities, translate the problem into mathematical language, use key words to guide your equation setup, and ensure consistency and simplicity in your equations.

Figure 6.2.2.1: Ways to Form Linear Equations in Two Variables

Example

(a) You're managing a pet store that sells both dogs and cats. You need to calculate the total number of pets in the store's inventory, considering that there are (d) dogs and (c) cats. Each dog occupies one kennel, and each cat occupies half a kennel. How would you express this situation algebraically?

Step 1: Identify the components:

  • We have (d) dogs and (c) cats in the store's inventory.

Step 2: Define the expression:

  • The total number of kennels occupied by pets in the store can be algebraically expressed as (d + 0.5c).

Step 3: Explanation of Expression:

  • Each dog occupies one kennel, so we add (d) to the total. Each cat occupies half a kennel, so we add (0.5c) to account for the cats.

Step 4: Example Calculation:

  • If there are 20 dogs (d = 20) and 15 cats (c = 15), then the total number of kennels occupied by pets in the store would be:

    Total kennels = 20 + 0.5 * 15 = 20 + 7.5 = 27.5 kennels

keyboard_arrow_right 6.2.3 How do you determine the possible solutions of linear equations in two variables?

Smart Tips

  • To find the product of algebraic expressionswith one term, gather all the same variables, and then multiply the number with number and the variable with variable.

Figure 6.2.3.1: Determine the Possible Solutions of Linear Equations in Two Variables

Example

(a) Let's consider a scenario where you're managing a community garden project. One volunteer spends twice as much time as another volunteer planting flowers. The first volunteer spends (y) hours planting flowers, while the second volunteer spends (x) hours. If the total time spent planting flowers by both volunteers is 6 hours, how would you determine the possible solutions to this situation?

Step 1: Identify the Components:

  • The total time spent planting flowers is the sum of the time spent by each volunteer, with the constraint that the total time must be 6 hours.

Step 2: Formulate the Equation:

  • Write the equation representing the constraint: 2y + x = 6.

Step 3: Determine the Possible Solutions using Substitution:

  • When x = 0,
    2y + 0 = 6,
    2y = 6,
    y =3
  • When x = 1,
    2y + 1 = 6,
    2y = 5,
    y = 2.5
  • When x = 3,
    2y + 3 = 6,
    2y = 3,
    y = 1.5
  • Thus, the three possible pair of solutions are
    (0, 3), (1, 2.5), and (3, 1.5).

Step 3: Formulate simultaneous linear equations:

  • The relationship between production quantities and costs can be represented by the following equations:

    2x + 3y = Total cost of producing Product A

keyboard_arrow_right 6.3.2 How do you solve simultaneous linear equations in two variables?

Three Methods to Solve Simultaneous Linear Equations in Two Variables

  • Graphical Method - The graphical method involves plotting the equations on a graph to locate the intersection point(s), which represent the solutions to the system of equations.

  • Substitution Method - The substitution method requires solving one equation for one variable, substituting this expression into the other equation, and solving for the remaining variable.

  • Elimination Method - The elimination method entails adding or subtracting equations to eliminate one variable, then solving the resulting equation for the remaining variable.

Figure 6.3.2.1: Graphical Method


Figure 6.3.2.2: Substitution Method


Figure 6.3.2.3: Elimination Method

Example

(a) You're managing your daily expenses and need to balance your budget between two categories: groceries and transportation. Let's denote the amount spent on groceries as (G) and the amount spent on transportation as (T). Formulate a system of two simultaneous linear equations to represent the relationship between your expenses and your budget constraints. Then, solve the system using graphical, substitution, and elimination methods.

Step 1: Identify the quantities:

  • Quantity 1: Amount spent on groceries (G).
  • Quantity 2: Amount spent on transportation (T).

Step 2: Define the variables:

  • Let (G) represent the amount spent on groceries.
  • Let (T) represent the amount spent on transportation.

Step 3: Formulate simultaneous linear equations:

  • We can represent the relationship between expenses and budget constraints with the following equations:

    [4G + 3T = 100] (Equation 1: Budget constraint for groceries and transportation)
    [2G - T = 20] (Equation 2: Relation between groceries and transportation expenses)

Step 4: Solve the system of equations using different methods:

  • Graphical Method:
    Plot the equations on a graph and find the intersection point(s) to determine the solution. 
                                        Equation 1: 4G + 3T = 100
                                    Equation 2: 2G - T = 20

                                    Solve for T:
                                    T1 = (100 - 4G) / 3
                                    T2 = 2G - 20

                                    Plot these equations and find the intersection point.
                                    Intersection point: (G, T) = (16, 12)
  • Substitution Method:
    From Equation 2 (2G - T = 20), solve for (T): 
                                        T = 2G - 20

                                    Substitute T into Equation 1 (4G + 3T = 100):
                                    4G + 3(2G - 20) = 100
                                    4G + 6G - 60 = 100
                                    10G - 60 = 100
                                    10G = 160
                                    G = 16

                                    Substitute G back into Equation 2:
                                    2(16) - T = 20
                                    32 - T = 20
                                    T = 12

                                    Solution: (G, T) = (16, 12)
  • Elimination Method:
    Multiply Equation 2 by 3 to eliminate (T): 
                                        3 * (2G - T) = 3 * 20
                                    6G - 3T = 60

                                    Add this modified Equation 2 to Equation 1 (4G + 3T = 100):
                                    4G + 3T + 6G - 3T = 100 + 60
                                    10G = 160
                                    G = 16

                                    Substitute G back into Equation 2:
                                    2(16) - T = 20
                                    32 - T = 20
                                    T = 12

                                    Solution: (G, T) = (16, 12)
