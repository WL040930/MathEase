keyboard_arrow_right 1.1.1 What are positive numbers and negative numbers?

Positive Numbers vs Negative Numbers

  • Positive Numbers - A positive number is a number greater than zero. It is denoted by a plus sign (+) before the number.

  • Negative Numbers - A negative number is a number less than zero. It is denoted by a minus sign (-) before the number.

Figure 1.1.1.1: Recognize Positive and Negative Numbers based on Real-Life Situations

Example

(a) You are comparing the temperatures of two cities. City A has a temperature of 15 degrees, and City B has a temperature of -5 degrees. Determine the difference in temperature between the two cities.

Step 1: Identify the values:

  • Temperature of City A: 15 degrees.
  • Temperature of City B: -5 degrees.

Step 2: Define the variable and equation:

  • Let TA represent the temperature of City A.
  • Let TB represent the temperature of City B.
  • The equation to find the difference in temperature is: TA - TB.

Step 3: Calculate the difference:

  • Substitute the given values into the equation: 15 - (-5).
  • Simplify the equation: 15 + 5 = 20.

Conclusion:

  • The difference in temperature between City A and City B is 20 degrees.

keyboard_arrow_right 1.1.2 What are integers?

Integers

  • Integers - An integer is a whole number that can be positive, negative, or zero. It includes all the positive numbers, negative numbers, and zero. For instance, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3 are all integers.

Figure 1.1.2.1: Recognize and Describe Integers

Example

(a) You are organizing a classroom activity to help students understand integers. You have a set of numbers that include both positive and negative values. Let's say the set of numbers is {15, -8, 0, 23, -5, -12, 7}. Determine which of these numbers are positive integers, which are negative integers, and identify if there are any non-integer values.

Step 1: Identify the positive integers:

  • Positive integers are greater than zero.
  • In the set {15, -8, 0, 23, -5, -12, 7}, the positive integers are: 15, 23, and 7.

Step 2: Identify the negative integers:

  • Negative integers are less than zero.
  • In the set {15, -8, 0, 23, -5, -12, 7}, the negative integers are: -8, -5, and -12.

Step 3: Identify any non-integer values:

  • Non-integer values are numbers that are not whole numbers (they include fractions or decimals).
  • In the set {15, -8, 0, 23, -5, -12, 7}, there are no non-integer values.

Step 4: Identify zero:

  • Zero is an integer, but it is neither positive nor negative.
  • In the set {15, -8, 0, 23, -5, -12, 7}, the number 0 is the zero integer.

Step 5: Final summary:

  • Positive integers: 15, 23, 7.
  • Negative integers: -8, -5, -12.
  • Zero: 0.
  • No non-integer values in the set.

keyboard_arrow_right 1.2.2 How do you divide and multiply integers?

Rules of Multiplication and Division of Integers

  • The product or quotient of two integers with the same signs is a positive integer.

  • The product or quotient of two integers with different signs is a negative integer.

Figure 1.2.2.1: Rules of Multiplication and Division of Integers

Example

(a) Imagine you are learning the rules of multiplication and division of integers. Define two variables to represent two different integers, and demonstrate the steps to multiply and divide these integers.

Step 1: Identify the components:

  • Let m represent the first integer. For example, let m = -8.
  • Let n represent the second integer. For example, let n = 4.

Step 2: Define the multiplication and division expressions:

  • Multiplication of the integers: P = m * n
  • Division of the first integer by the second: Q = m / n

Step 3: Explanation of Expressions:

  • Multiplication: When multiplying two integers, the result is positive if both integers have the same sign, and negative if they have different signs.
  • Division: When dividing two integers, the result follows the same rule regarding the signs as multiplication.

Step 4: Example Calculation:

  • Using the example values m = -8 and n = 4:
  • Multiplication: P = -8 * 4 = -32
  • Division: Q = -8 / 4 = -2

keyboard_arrow_right 1.2.3 How do you describe the laws of arithmetic operation?

Laws of Arithmetic Operations

  • Commutative Law - The commutative law states that the order of numbers in an arithmetic operation does not affect the result. For example, a + b = b + a.

  • Associative Law - The associative law states that the grouping of numbers in an arithmetic operation does not affect the result. For example, (a + b) + c = a + (b + c).

  • Distributive Law - The distributive law states that the product of a number and the sum of two numbers is equal to the sum of the products of the number and each of the two numbers. For example, a * (b + c) = a * b + a * c.

  • Identity Law - The identity law states that the sum of any number and zero is the number itself. For example, a + 0 = a.

Figure 1.2.3.1: Laws of Arithmetic Operations

Example

(a) Let's apply the Laws of Arithmetic Operations to solve the expression (5 + 3) * 2.

Step 1: Initial Expression:

  • Start with the given expression: (5 + 3) * 2.

Step 2: Apply the Commutative Law:

  • According to the Commutative Law, the order of numbers in multiplication does not affect the result.
  • We can rearrange the expression: (3 + 5) * 2.

Step 3: Apply the Associative Law:

  • The Associative Law states that the grouping of numbers in an arithmetic operation does not affect the result.
  • We can group the numbers differently: 3 + (5 * 2).

Step 4: Perform the Operations:

  • Calculate the expressions: (3 + 5) = 8, and (5 * 2) = 10.
  • Multiply the result of the addition by 2: 8 * 2 = 16.

Step 5: Final Result:

  • The final result of the expression (5 + 3) * 2 is 16.

  • Perform computations involving combined basic arithmetic operations of positive and negative fractions by following the order of operations.

Scenario

(a) Imagine you're planning a recipe for a special dish. You need to combine different ingredients in specific quantities. However, you want to adjust the recipe to make it suitable for a larger group of guests. Let's say you need to calculate the total quantity of one particular ingredient by performing some arithmetic operations.

Step 1: Calculate the Total Quantity:

  • You start with 2/3 cup of the ingredient.
  • Next, you add 1/6 cup more to enhance the flavor.
  • Then, you realize you've added too much, so you decide to subtract 5/2 cups from the total.

Step 2: Perform Arithmetic Operations:

  • First, add 2/3 and 1/6 together: 2/3 + 1/6 = 4/6 + 1/6 = 5/6.
  • Next, subtract 5/2 from the result: 5/6 - 5/2.
  • Convert 5/2 to a fraction with a denominator of 6: 5/2 = 15/6.
  • Subtract 15/6 from 5/6: 5/6 - 15/6 = -10/6.
  • Simplify -10/6 to its simplest form: -10/6 = -5/3.

Step 3: Final Result:

  • The final quantity of the ingredient is -5/3 cups.

Example

Step 1: Calculate Total Income from Tickets:

  • If you sell 10 tickets, the income would be 10 * $3.50 = $35.00.
  • If you sell 20 tickets, the income would be 20 * $3.50 = $70.00.

Step 2: Determine if the Income Covers the Costs:

  • Check if $35.00 (from 10 tickets) covers $50.75 in costs. No, it does not.
  • Check if $70.00 (from 20 tickets) covers $50.75 in costs. Yes, it does.

Step 3: Calculate the Exact Number of Tickets:

  • Start by finding the minimum number of tickets where income exceeds costs: $50.75 / $3.50 = approximately 14.5.
  • Since you can't sell half a ticket, round up to the next whole number: 15 tickets.

Step 4: Confirm the Result:

  • Calculate the income from selling 15 tickets: 15 * $3.50 = $52.50.
  • Since $52.50 is more than $50.75, selling 15 tickets will cover your costs.

Final Answer:

  • You need to sell at least 15 tickets to cover your costs.

keyboard_arrow_right 1.5 Rational Numbers

keyboard_arrow_right 1.5.1 What are rational numbers?

Rational Numbers

  • Rational Numbers - A rational number is a number that can be expressed as a fraction where the numerator and denominator are integers, and the denominator is not zero. Rational numbers include integers, fractions, and decimals that terminate or repeat.

Figure 1.5.1.1: Recognize and Describe Rational Numbers

Example

(a) Imagine you're helping to organize a food fair. You have different types of snacks priced in fractions and decimals. You want to check if the prices of these snacks are rational numbers. How would you do this?

Step 1: Identify the Snack Prices:

  • Snack 1 costs $2.75.
  • Snack 2 costs $1/3 (one-third of a dollar).
  • Snack 3 costs $4.50.
  • Snack 4 costs $0.333... (repeating decimal).

Step 2: Determine if Each Price is a Rational Number:

  • Price $2.75: This is a decimal that terminates, so it is a rational number.
  • Price $1/3: This is a fraction, so it is a rational number.
  • Price $4.50: This is a decimal that terminates, so it is a rational number.
  • Price $0.333...: This is a repeating decimal, so it is a rational number.

Step 3: Conclusion:

  • All the prices ($2.75, $1/3, $4.50, and $0.333...) are rational numbers because they can be expressed as either a fraction or a terminating/repeating decimal.

Smart Tips

  • Perform computations involving combined basic arithmetic operations of rational numbers by following the order of operations.

Figure 1.5.2.1: Arithmetic Operations of Rational Numbers

Example

(a) You are planning a recipe and need to calculate the total amount of ingredients you will use. Let's say the amount of flour required is 2/3 cup. The amount of sugar required is 1/4 cup more than half the amount of flour. Finally, the amount of butter required is 1/2 cup less than twice the amount of sugar. Calculate the total amount of ingredients you will use in cups.

Step 1: Define the quantities:

  • The amount of flour required is 2/3 cup.
  • The amount of sugar required is (1/2 × 2/3 + 1/4) cup.
  • The amount of butter required is (2 × (1/2 × 2/3 + 1/4) - 1/2) cup.

Step 2: Calculate each amount:

  • The amount of sugar is (1/2 × 2/3 + 1/4) = (1/3 + 1/4) = 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12 cup.
  • The amount of butter is (2 × 7/12 - 1/2) = 14/12 - 6/12 = 8/12 = 2/3 cup.

Step 3: Calculate the total amount of ingredients:

  • The total amount is the sum of the amounts of flour, sugar, and butter.
  • So, the total amount is (2/3 + 7/12 + 2/3).
  • Converting to a common denominator: (2/3 = 8/12), so (8/12 + 7/12 + 8/12) = 23/12 cups.

Step 4: Final calculation:

  • The total amount of ingredients you will use is 23/12 cups or 1 11/12 cups.
