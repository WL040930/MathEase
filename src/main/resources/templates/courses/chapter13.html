keyboard_arrow_right What is a hypotenuse?

Hypotenuse

The hypotenuse is a term used in geometry, specifically in the context of right-angled triangles. A right-angled triangle is a triangle that has one angle measuring 90 degrees.

Key Points:

  • Right-Angled Triangle: This type of triangle has one angle that is exactly 90 degrees.
  • Sides of a Right-Angled Triangle:
    • Legs: The two sides that form the right angle.
    • Hypotenuse: The side opposite the right angle and the longest side of the triangle.

Right-Angled Triangle 2:

keyboard_arrow_right What is the relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle?

Pythagorean Theorem

The relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle is described by the Pythagorean theorem. This fundamental principle in geometry states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (c) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides (a and b), which are referred to as the legs of the triangle.

Figure 13.1.2.1: Example of a Right Angled Triangle

Explanation

The Pythagorean theorem tells us that in a right-angled triangle:

  • The area of the square on the longest side (R) is the same as the combined areas of the squares on the other two sides (P and Q).

  • R2 = P2 + Q2

This is a fundamental rule in geometry for right-angled triangles.

Equation

a2 + b2 = c2

Where:

  • a and b are the lengths of the legs.
  • c is the length of the hypotenuse.

Example

We have a triangle with sides labeled as ‘a’, ‘b’, and ‘c’. Let's find the hypotenuse.

Figure 13.1.2.2: Example Triangle

Step 1: Identify the Hypotenuse

  • The side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse. Let’s assume that side ‘c’ represents the hypotenuse.

Step 2: Apply the Pythagorean Theorem

  • The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right triangle, the sum of the squares of the two legs (sides ‘a’ and ‘b’) is equal to the square of the hypotenuse (side ‘c’): a2 + b2 = c2

Step 3: Solving for Unknown Side Length

  • If you know the lengths of any two sides, you can use the equation to find the length of the third side.

Step 4: Substitute Known Values

  • Substitute the known values into the Pythagorean theorem: c = √(a2 + b2)

Step 5: Calculate Hypotenuse Length

  • Calculate the square root of the sum of the squares of ‘a’ and ‘b’ to find the length of the hypotenuse ‘c’.

keyboard_arrow_right How do you determine whether a triangle is a right-angled triangle?

Triangle

Triangle can be classified into three types based on the angles:

  • Acute Triangle: All angles are less than 90 degrees.
  • Obtuse Triangle: One angle is greater than 90 degrees.
  • Right-Angled Triangle: One angle is exactly 90 degrees.

Figure 13.2.1.1: Acute Triangle

Figure 13.2.1.2: Obtuse Triangle

Figure 13.2.1.3: Right Angled Triangle

In a right-angled triangle, one of the angles is a right angle, meaning it measures exactly 90 degrees. This angle is formed by the intersection of the two shorter sides of the triangle, known as the legs. The other two angles in a right-angled triangle are acute angles, meaning they are less than 90 degrees.

Identifying a Right-Angled Triangle:

To identify a right-angled triangle, you can look for the following:

  • Angle Measurement:

    Check if one of the angles in the triangle measures exactly 90 degrees. If it does, then it's a right-angled triangle.

  • Pythagorean Theorem:

    Apply the Pythagorean theorem, which states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides. If this theorem holds true for the triangle in question, then it is a right-angled triangle.

Example

Determine whether each of the following triangles is a right-angled triangle

Figure 13.2.1.4: A triangle

Solution Steps:

Step 1: Identify the sides and their lengths

  • Given:
    • SU = 20 cm
    • ST = 12 cm
    • TU = 18 cm

Step 2: Check if it satisfies the Pythagorean theorem

  • Calculate the squares of the lengths of each side:
    • SU2 = 202 = 400
    • ST2 = 122 = 144
    • TU2 = 182 = 324
  • Check if the sum of the squares of the two shorter sides equals the square of the longest side:
    • SU2 + ST2 = 400 + 144 = 544
    • TU2 = 324
  • Since 544 is not equal to 324, the triangle does not satisfy the Pythagorean theorem.

Step 3: Conclusion

  • Since the triangle does not satisfy the Pythagorean theorem, it is not a right-angled triangle.

Step 4: Additional Note (Optional)

  • If you want to find out the type of triangle it is, you can compare the lengths of the sides and determine whether it is an acute-angled, obtuse-angled, or scalene triangle.

Step 2: Calculate angle ∠PQS:

Since it's a right-angled triangle at point Q, angle ∠PQS equals 90°.

Step 3: Calculate angle ∠SQR:

Use the angle sum property:

∠SQR = 90° - 15° = 75°

Question

Sheila is given three straws to form a frame in the shape of a right-angled triangle. The straws are 15 cm, 20 cm and 25 cm long respectively. Will she be able to form the frame in the shape of a right-angled triangle?

Solution

Step 1: Identify the Sides

Given straws:

  • a = 15 cm
  • b = 20 cm
  • c = 25 cm (assuming c is the hypotenuse because it is the longest side)

Step 2: Apply the Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean theorem states:

c2 = a2 + b2

Step 3: Calculate the Squares of the Sides

  • a2 = 152 = 225
  • b2 = 202 = 400
  • c2 = 252 = 625

Step 4: Check the Pythagorean Theorem

Check if c2 equals a2 + b2:

625 = 225 + 400

625 = 625

Step 5: Conclusion

Since the equation holds true (625 = 625), the straws of lengths 15 cm, 20 cm, and 25 cm can indeed form a right-angled triangle.
