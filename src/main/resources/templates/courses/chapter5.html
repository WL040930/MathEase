keyboard_arrow_right 5.1.1 How do you use letters to represent variables?

Variable

  • Variables - It can be defined by symbols that represent numbers. Instead of using a specific number, we use a letter or symbol to stand in for the unknown value. For example, in the equation x + 3 = 7, x is a variable.

  • When to use - Variables are used when we don't know the exact value, but we want to talk about it or perform mathematical operations with it. They allow us to express relationships and solve problems without knowing the specific numbers involved.

keyboard_arrow_right 5.1.3 How do you determine the values from algebraic expression?

Smart Tips

  • The value of an algebraic expression can be determined by substituting the variables with the given values.

  • Determine the values of algebraic expressions given the value of variables and make connections with appropriate situations.

Figure 5.1.3.1: Determine the Values from Algebraic Expression

Example

(a) Imagine you're organizing a school event and need to calculate the total cost of supplies. Let's consider two types of supplies: markers and notebooks. Define two variables to represent the number of markers and the number of notebooks you'll purchase, respectively.

Step 1: Identify the quantities:

  • Quantity 1: Number of markers.
  • Quantity 2: Number of notebooks.

Step 2: Define the variables:

  • Let (m) represent the number of markers you'll buy.
  • Let (n) represent the number of notebooks you'll buy.

Step 3: Define the algebraic expression:

  • Suppose the cost of one marker is $1.50 and the cost of one notebook is $2.00.
  • The total cost (C) can be represented as: C = 1.50m + 2.00n.

Step 4: Determine the total cost:

  • If you plan to buy 10 markers and 5 notebooks, substitute the values into the expression:
    C = 1.50(10) + 2.00(5)
    C = 15 + 10
    C = 25
    Therefore, the total cost is $25.

keyboard_arrow_right 5.1.4 What are the terms and coefficient in an expression?

Terms

  • Terms - Terms in algebraic expressions are individual parts separated by addition or subtraction signs. For example, in 3x^2 + 5x - 7, the terms are 3x^2, 5x, and -7.

  • Coefficients - Coefficients are the numbers multiplying the variables within those terms. Based on the example given above, in 3x^2, the coefficient is 3, and in 5x, the coefficient is 5.

Figure 5.1.4.1: Determine the Terms and Coefficient in an Expression

Example

(a) Consider the expression (2x^2 - 3xy + 5). Identify the terms and coefficients in this expression.

Step 1: Identify Terms:

  • Term 1: (2x^2)
  • Term 2: (-3xy)
  • Term 3: (5)

Step 2: Determine Coefficients:

  • For (2x^2), the coefficient is (2).
  • For (3xy), the coefficient is (-3).
  • For the constant term (5), the coefficient is (5).

keyboard_arrow_right 5.1.5 What are like terms and unlike terms?

Like Terms vs Unlike Terms

  • Like Terms - Like terms are terms in algebra that share the same variable(s) raised to the same power(s). They can be combined or simplified together. For example, 3x and 5x are like terms because they both have the variable x raised to the power of 1.

  • Unlike Terms - Unlike terms are terms in algebra that have different variable(s) or the same variable(s) raised to different power(s). They cannot be combined directly. For example, 3x and 5y are unlike terms because they have different variables, while 3x and 3x^2 are also unlike terms because they have the same variable but raised to different powers.

Figure 5.1.5.1: Difference between Like Terms and Unlike Terms

Example

(a) Consider the expression (3x^2 - 2xy + 4x - 5y + 7). Identify the like terms and unlike terms in this expression.

Step 1: Identify Like Terms and Unlike Terms:

  • Like Terms: Terms that have the same variables raised to the same powers.
    • Like Term 1: (3x^2)
    • Like Term 2: (4x)
  • Unlike Terms: Terms that have different variables or the same variables raised to different powers.
    • Unlike Term 1: (-2xy)
    • Unlike Term 2: (-5y)
    • Unlike Term 3: (7)

keyboard_arrow_right 5.2.1 How do you add and substract two more more algebraic expressions?

Smart Tips

  • When the "+" sign which lies before the brackets is removed, the sign for each term in the brackets remains unchanged.

  • When the "-" sign which lies before the brackets is removed, the sign for each term in the brackets changes from "+ to -" and from "- to +".

Figure 5.2.1.1: Effects of Removing Signs Before Brackets on Terms within Algebraic Expressions

Example

(a) Consider the algebraic expressions (3x + 2y - 5) and (4x - 3y + 7). How do you add and subtract these expressions?

Step 1: Identify the expressions:

  • Expression 1: (3x + 2y - 5)
  • Expression 2: (4x - 3y + 7)

Step 2: Perform Addition:

Add corresponding terms together:

  • For x terms: (3x + 4x) = 7x
  • For y terms: (2y - 3y) = -y
  • For constant terms: (-5 + 7) = 2

Step 3: Perform Subtraction:

Subtract the second expression from the first:

  • For x terms: [3x - (+4x)] = -x
  • For y terms: [2y -(-3y)] = 5y
  • For constant terms: [-5 - (+7)] = -12

keyboard_arrow_right 5.2.3 How do you multiply and divide algebraic expressions?

Smart Tips

  • To find the product of algebraic expressionswith one term, gather all the same variables, and then multiply the number with number and the variable with variable.

Figure 5.2.3.1: Simplify the Expressions

Example

(a) Imagine you're organizing a science fair and need to calculate the total number of experiments conducted by each participant. One participant performs (3x) experiments, another performs (2y) experiments, and a third performs (5z) experiments. How would you algebraically express the total number of experiments conducted by all participants?

Step 1: Identify the Components:

  • The total number of experiments is determined by the sum of experiments conducted by each participant, represented by (3x), (2y), and (5z) experiments, respectively.

Step 2: Determine the Operations:

  • We need to multiply each participant's number of experiments to find the total.

Step 3: Simplify the Expression:

  • To simplify, we'll multiply the number of experiments performed by each participant together. For instance, (3x) experiments multiplied by (2y) experiments, and then multiplied by (5z) experiments.

Step 4: Perform Multiplication:

  • First, we multiply (3x) by (2y), resulting in (6xy) experiments. Then, we multiply this result by (5z), yielding (30xyz) experiments.

Step 5: Final Expression:

  • The total number of experiments conducted by all participants can be expressed algebraically as (30xyz) experiments.
