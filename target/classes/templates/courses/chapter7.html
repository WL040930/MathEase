keyboard_arrow_right 7.1.1 What are inequalities?

Inequalities

  • Inequalities - Inequalities are mathematical expressions used to compare two values or expressions. Unlike equations, which assert that two expressions are equal, inequalities show that one expression is larger or smaller than the other.

Example

(a) You're organizing a sports event and need to ensure that the number of participants in two different activities, soccer and basketball, meets certain criteria. Define two variables to represent the number of participants in soccer and basketball, respectively, and use inequalities to compare the values.

Step 1: Identify the quantities:

  • Quantity 1: Number of participants in soccer.
  • Quantity 2: Number of participants in basketball.

Step 2: Define the variables:

  • Let (s) represent the number of participants in soccer.
  • Let (b) represent the number of participants in basketball.

Step 3: Formulate inequalities to compare the values:

  • If you want at least twice as many participants in soccer as in basketball, write the inequality: s >= 2b.
  • If you want fewer than 100 participants in soccer, write the inequality: s < 100.
  • If the total number of participants in both activities should not exceed 150, write the inequality: s + b <= 150.

Step 4: Provide examples of inequalities:

  • For example, if you have 50 participants in basketball, the number of participants in soccer should be at least 100 (s >= 2b).
  • For example, if you have 80 participants in soccer, the number of participants in basketball should be less than 100 (b < 100).
  • For example, if you have 100 participants in soccer, the total number of participants in both activities should not exceed 150 (s + b <= 150).

keyboard_arrow_right 7.1.2 What are the properties of inequalities?

Smart Tips

  • Additive Inverse Property - For any real number (a), adding its additive inverse, denoted as (-a), yields zero: [a + (-a) = 0]. In inequalities, adding the additive inverse of a number to both sides maintains the inequality's direction.

  • Multiplicative Inverse Property - For any non-zero real number (a), multiplying it by its multiplicative inverse (1/a) results in one: (a x 1/a = 1). In inequalities, multiplying both sides by the multiplicative inverse of a positive number preserves the inequality's direction.

Example

(a) You have a budget for groceries and transportation. Let's denote the amount spent on groceries as G and the amount spent on transportation as T. Your budget constraints are defined by two inequalities: 4G + 3T ≤ 100 (representing the maximum budget for groceries and transportation combined) and 2G - T ≥ 20 (representing the minimum budget for groceries compared to transportation). Using the properties of additive and multiplicative inverses, solve the system of inequalities to determine the feasible range of expenses for groceries (G) and transportation (T).

Step 1: Identify the quantities:

  • Quantity 1: Amount spent on groceries (G).
  • Quantity 2: Amount spent on transportation (T).

Step 2: Formulate the inequalities:

  • The budget constraints are represented by the following inequalities:

    4G + 3T ≤ 100 (Maximum budget for groceries and transportation combined)
    2G - T ≥ 20 (Minimum budget for groceries compared to transportation)

Step 3: Solve the system of inequalities:

  • Additive Inverse Property: To isolate G or T, apply the additive inverse by adding or subtracting the same value from both sides of the inequality.

    Example: Given 4G + 3T ≤ 100, subtract 4G from both sides to isolate 3T:
    4G + 3T - 4G ≤ 100 - 4G
    3T ≤ 100 - 4G

    Similarly, apply the additive inverse to 2G - T ≥ 20 to isolate T.

  • Multiplicative Inverse Property: To solve for G or T, apply the multiplicative inverse by multiplying or dividing both sides of the inequality by the same positive or negative value.

    Example: If we have 3T ≤ 100 - 4G, to solve for T, we can divide both sides by 3:
    (1/3) * (3T) ≤ (1/3) * (100 - 4G)
    T ≤ (100 - 4G) / 3

    Similarly, apply the multiplicative inverse to solve for G in the inequality 2G - T ≥ 20.

Step 4: Interpret the solution:

  • To interpret the solution, let's first find the feasible range of expenses for groceries (G) and transportation (T).

    Example:
    - For the inequality 3T ≤ 100 - 4G, we have T ≤ (100 - 4G) / 3.
    - Similarly, for 2G - T ≥ 20, we can solve for G to get G ≥ (T + 20) / 2.

    Now, to find the feasible range, we need to consider both inequalities together.

  • Considering both inequalities:
    - We need to find the intersection of the feasible ranges obtained for G and T.
    - This intersection represents the values that satisfy both inequalities simultaneously, providing the feasible range of expenses for groceries and transportation.

    Example:
    Let's say we found that G can range from 10 to 30, and T can range from 20 to 40 based on the solutions of the inequalities.
    - This means that the feasible range for G is from 10 to 30, and for T is from 20 to 40.

keyboard_arrow_right 7.2 Linear Inequalities in One Variable

keyboard_arrow_right 7.2.1 How do you form linear inequalities based on the daily life situations and vice versa?

Smart Tips

  • To form linear inequalities from daily life situations, identify quantities, assign variables, establish relationships, and express constraints using inequality symbols. Conversely, interpret linear inequalities in real-life scenarios by understanding their implications within the context of the situation and the relationships between the variables involved.

Figure 7.2.1.1: Example Usage of Linear Inequalities


Figure 7.2.1.2: Solving Linear Inequalities

Example

(a) Consider the following scenario: A company produces two types of products, Product A and Product B. Each unit of Product A requires 2 hours of labor and 3 hours of machine time, while each unit of Product B requires 1 hour of labor and 2 hours of machine time. The company has a maximum of 100 hours of labor and 120 hours of machine time available. How many units of each product should the company produce to maximize its profit?

Step 1: Define Variables:

  • Let x represent the number of units of Product A produced and y represent the number of units of Product B produced.

Step 2: Form Inequalities:

Based on the labor and machine time constraints, we form the following inequalities:

  • Labor Constraint: 2x + y ≤ 100 (Labor hours available)
  • Machine Time Constraint: 3x + 2y ≤ 120 (Machine hours available)

Step 3: Solve the Inequalities:

Let's solve the inequalities to find the feasible values for x and y.

  • Labor Constraint:
    • Substitute the given labor hours into the inequality:
    2x + y ≤ 100
    Solve for y:
    y ≤ 100 - 2x
  • Machine Time Constraint:
    • Substitute the given machine hours into the inequality:
    3x + 2y ≤ 120
    Solve for y:
    y ≤ (120 - 3x) / 2

Step 4: Determine Feasible Region:

The feasible region is the intersection of the solution sets of both inequalities.

We have two inequalities:

  • y ≤ 100 - 2x
  • y ≤ (120 - 3x) / 2

So, the feasible region is the region where both inequalities are satisfied simultaneously.

Step 5: Optimize Profit:

  • After finding the feasible region, we optimize profit by evaluating the objective function (profit) at each corner point of the feasible region.

keyboard_arrow_right 7.2.2 How do you solve simultaneous linear inequalities?

Smart Tips

  • To solve simultaneous linear inequalities, graph each one on a coordinate plane, shading the areas where the inequalities are true. The overlapping shaded regions represent where all inequalities are satisfied simultaneously. These areas represent the solution set.

Figure 7.2.2.1: Solving Simultaneous Linear Inequalities

Example

(a) You're managing a manufacturing plant that produces two types of products, Product X and Product Y. Each product requires certain resources, represented by the variables "x" and "y". You have constraints on the availability of these resources, given by the inequalities "2x + 6 >= 10" and "5x + 3 < 15". How would you express and solve this situation algebraically?

Step 1: Identify the Inequalities:

We have two inequalities representing constraints on the availability of resources:

  • Inequality 1: 2x + 6 ≥ 10
  • Inequality 2: 5x + 3 < 15

Step 2: Solve the Inequalities:

Let's solve each inequality to find the feasible values for x.

  • Inequality 1:
    • Subtract 6 from both sides:
    2x ≥ 4

    Divide both sides by 2:
    x ≥ 2
  • Inequality 2:
    • Subtract 3 from both sides:
    5x < 12

    Divide both sides by 5:
    x < 12/5

Step 3: Determine Feasible Region:

The feasible region is the intersection of the solution sets of both inequalities.

  • From Inequality 1: x ≥ 2
  • From Inequality 2: x < 12/5
  • So, the feasible region is 2 ≤ x < 12/5.

Step 4: Final Solution:

  • The final solution set for x is 2 ≤ x < 12/5.
