Understanding Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression is a mathematical phrase that consists of variables, numbers, and arithmetic operations. It represents a relationship or rule without knowing specific values. Here are some key points about algebraic expressions:

  • Variables: Represented by letters, variables can stand for unknown or changing quantities.
  • Coefficients: Numbers multiplied by variables are coefficients.
  • Terms: Parts of an expression separated by addition or subtraction.
  • Operations: Common operations include addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

Algebraic expressions are fundamental in mathematics, used to model real-world situations, formulate equations, and solve problems.

Example

Consider a scenario where you have 3 bags of marbles. Let's call the number of marbles in the first bag "x", the second bag contains 5 more marbles than the first bag, and the third bag has twice the number of marbles in the second bag. Write an algebraic expression to represent the total number of marbles you have in terms of "x".

Step 1: Define the variables:

  • Let (x) represent the number of marbles in the first bag.
  • The second bag contains (x + 5) marbles (5 more than the first bag).
  • The third bag has 2 times (x + 5) marbles (twice the number in the second bag).

Step 2: Write the algebraic expression for the total number of marbles:

  • The total number of marbles is the sum of marbles in all three bags.
  • So, the expression is [x + (x + 5) + 2 ร (x + 5)].
  • Simplifying, we get (x + x + 5 + 2x + 10).
  • Combining like terms, the expression becomes (4x + 15).

Step 3: Final expression:

  • The final algebraic expression representing the total number of marbles is (4x + 15).
  • This expression gives us the total number of marbles based on the number of marbles in the first bag represented by (x).

keyboard_arrow_right 4.1.2 How do you express ratios in their simplest form?

Expressing Ratios

  • Ratios - Ratios express the relationship between two or more quantities. They can be written in the form a:b, where a and b are numbers representing the quantities being compared.

  • To express ratios in their simplest form, it's necessary to divide both parts of the ratio by their greatest common divisor (GCD). This ensures that the ratio cannot be further simplified.

Figure 4.1.2.1: Illustration of Simplifying Ratios


Example

(a) You have a bag of marbles with a ratio of blue marbles to red marbles of 3:5. Express this ratio in its simplest form.

Step 1: Write the ratio:

  • The given ratio is 3:5.

Step 2: Find the greatest common divisor (GCD):

  • The GCD of 3 and 5 is 1, as there are no common factors other than 1.

Step 3: Divide both parts of the ratio by the GCD:

  • Dividing 3 by 1 and 5 by 1, we get the simplest form of the ratio, which is 3:5.

Relationship between Ratios and Rates

  • Ratios - Ratios express the relationship between two or more quantities. They are typically written in the form a:b.

  • Rates - Rates are special kinds of ratios that involve a comparison of two different kinds of quantities. They are often expressed in the form of a ratio where one quantity is measured against another with different units, such as miles per hour or dollars per pound.

  • The relationship between ratios and rates lies in the fact that rates are essentially a specific type of ratio. Ratios compare similar quantities, while rates compare quantities with different units or measures.

Figure 4.2.1.1: Illustration of Relationship between Ratios and Rates


Example

(a) You travel 200 miles in 4 hours. What is your rate of travel in miles per hour?

Step 1: Write the ratio:

  • The ratio of distance traveled to time taken is 200:4.

Step 2: Calculate the rate:

  • To find the rate, divide the distance traveled (200 miles) by the time taken (4 hours).
  • So, the rate of travel is 200 miles รท 4 hours = 50 miles per hour.

keyboard_arrow_right 4.3.1 What is the relationship between ratios and proportions?

Relationship between Ratios and Proportions

  • Ratios - Ratios express the relationship between two or more quantities. They are typically written in the form a:b.

  • Proportions - Proportions are statements that two ratios are equal. They are typically written in the form a:b = c:d.

  • The relationship between ratios and proportions lies in the fact that proportions are essentially a comparison of ratios. In a proportion, the relationship between the terms of one ratio is equal to the relationship between the terms of another ratio.

Figure 4.3.1.1: Illustration of Relationship between Ratios and Proportions


Example

(a) If 2 oranges cost $3, how much do 5 oranges cost?

Step 1: Set up the proportion:

  • 2 oranges cost $3, so the ratio of oranges to cost is 2:3.
  • We need to find the cost of 5 oranges.
  • So, the proportion is 2:3 = 5:x.

Step 2: Solve for x:

  • To solve for x, cross multiply: 2 * x = 3 * 5.
  • This gives us 2x = 15.
  • Divide both sides by 2: x = 7.5.

Step 3: Final answer:

  • The cost of 5 oranges is $7.5.

keyboard_arrow_right 4.3.2 How do you determine the unknown value in a proportion?

Determining Unknown Values in Proportions

  • Proportions - Proportions are statements that two ratios are equal. They are typically written in the form a:b = c:d.

  • To determine the unknown value in a proportion, you can use the method of cross multiplication.

  • Cross multiplication involves multiplying the numerator of one ratio by the denominator of the other ratio, and vice versa.

Figure 4.3.2.1: Illustration of Determining Unknown Values in Proportions


Example

(a) If 4 apples cost $2, how much do 6 apples cost?

Step 1: Set up the proportion:

  • For the given proportion, 4 apples cost $2, so the ratio of apples to cost is 4:2.
  • We need to find the cost of 6 apples.
  • So, the proportion is 4:2 = 6:x.

Step 2: Solve for x using cross multiplication:

  • Cross multiply: 4 * x = 2 * 6.
  • This gives us 4x = 12.
  • Divide both sides by 4: x = 3.

Step 3: Final answer:

  • The cost of 6 apples is $3.

keyboard_arrow_right 4.4.1 How do you determine the ratio of three quantities when two or more ratios of two quantities are given?

Determining Ratio of Three Quantities from Ratios of Two Quantities

  • When two or more ratios of two quantities are given, and you need to determine the ratio of three quantities, you can use a method called "equating the products of the means and extremes."

  • This method involves setting up equations where the product of the means (the inner terms) of the given ratios is equal to the product of the extremes (the outer terms) of the required ratio.

Example

(a) If the ratio of the number of apples to oranges is 3:4 and the ratio of oranges to bananas is 5:2, what is the ratio of apples to oranges to bananas?

Step 1: Equate the products of the means and extremes:

  • Given the ratios: Apples to Oranges = 3:4 and Oranges to Bananas = 5:2.
  • Setting up the equations, we have: (Apples) * (Bananas) = (Oranges) * (Oranges).

Step 2: Solve the equations:

  • From the given ratios, we find that the product of means in the first ratio (3 * 2) equals the product of extremes in the second ratio (4 * 5).
  • Therefore, the ratio of Apples to Oranges to Bananas is 3:4:5.

Determining Ratio or Related Value

  • If you're determining a related value, such as the cost, distance, or time, you may need to use proportions, equations, or other mathematical methods depending on the context of the problem.

Example

Step 1: Determine the intermediate ratio:

  • First, determine the ratio of blue marbles to act as an intermediary between red and green marbles.
  • From the given ratios, we have 2:3 for red to blue marbles and 5:4 for blue to green marbles.

Step 2: Calculate the combined ratio:

  • Multiply the ratios of red to blue and blue to green to get the ratio of red to green marbles.
